🔴 We're not filtering applicants at the start of most pipelines
8 out of 11 roles are calling almost every applicant for a screen. Best-in-class teams review 4–10 before picking up the phone. This usually means job descriptions are too vague, or there's no clear checklist of must-haves to filter against.
🔴 We're doing too many final interviews before making a decision
Director of Finance, Web Developer, and Marketing Intern all have 6+ final interviews for every offer made. Best-in-class is 2–3. That's double the work — and it risks good candidates accepting elsewhere while we're still deliberating.
✅ The mid-stage handoff from recruiter to hiring manager is mostly working
Most roles are passing the right number of candidates through to hiring managers. Business Dev Rep and Financial Analyst are right on track here. Account Executive is the one exception — 10 screened candidates per hiring manager interview, when 3–5 is the target.
💡 Financial Analyst is our best-performing pipeline — use it as the template
It's the only role hitting industry standards at two out of three checks. The recruiter and hiring manager are well-aligned, and the team makes offer decisions efficiently. The one gap is top-of-funnel volume — slightly broader sourcing would complete the picture.

Two surveys per hire: one sent to the hiring manager at the close of the hiring process (satisfaction with the process itself), and one at the 6-month mark (satisfaction with the hire's performance), plus 6-month retention and hiring manager eNPS. A lagging check on whether the leading indicators above are actually producing good hires.

💡 Early signal: weak pipelines correlate with weaker outcomes
Both hires who left before 6 months — Director of Finance and Digital Brand Manager — also scored lowest on the close-of-process survey, and came from pipelines flagged "needs attention" on multiple checks (too many final interviews, and an overly narrow job description). Our highest-satisfaction, highest-eNPS hires (Financial Analyst, Business Dev Rep) came from the healthiest pipelines and scored well at both survey points. Small sample so far, but worth tracking as we collect more data.

Every role has an approved salary budget. When a role closes, the gap between budget and actual offer feeds a shared Out-of-Budget (OOB) pool — savings top it up, overages draw it down.

💡 How the Out-of-Budget (OOB) pool works
We start each year with a $30,000 OOB contingency. When a role closes under budget, the savings go back into the pool. When a role closes over budget — for example, to win a strong candidate in a competitive process — it draws the pool down instead of needing a fresh approval. This gives hiring managers some flexibility on individual offers as long as the pool overall stays healthy.
